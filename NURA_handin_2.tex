\section{Satellite galaxies around a massive central}

\subsection{(1a) Determining the normalization}

Explain the integration method used (e.g. Romberg integration or another Richardson method).  
Describe how you implemented the spherical volume element \(dV\).

We will first solve the integral 
\begin{equation}
\int \int \int_V n(x) dV.
\end{equation}
Afterwards we will solve for A by requiring that the integral equals $\langle N_\mathrm{sat} \rangle$.
Note that
\begin{equation}
n(x) = A \langle N_\mathrm{sat} \rangle 
\left( \frac{x}{b}\right)^{a-3}
\exp\left[-\left(\frac{x}{b}\right)^c\right]
\end{equation}
does not depend on $\theta$ and  $\phi$. Using spherical coordinates turns this 3d integral into a 
1-dimensional integral which is a lot easier to solve numerically. 
The integral then becomes
\begin{equation}
4\pi\int_{10^{-5}}^5 
A \langle N_\mathrm{sat} \rangle 
\left( \frac{x}{b}\right)^{a-3}
\exp\left[-\left(\frac{x}{b}\right)^c\right]
x^2 dx.
\end{equation}
We will use Romberg integration as our method of numeric integration.
Romberg integration is a lot more accurate if the function is close to linear,
Since it uses evenly spaced samples and trapezoid to approximate the function. 
We split up the integral into 2 parts:
\begin{equation}
4\pi\int_{10^{-5}}^b 
n(x)
x^2 dx.
+ 
4\pi\int_{b}^5
n(x)
x^2 dx.
\end{equation}
In the first integral the $\left(\frac{x}{b}\right)$ term varies most rapidly. While in the second the exponential dominates the change in value of the integrand.  
For the first integral we rewrite the integral into an integral over logspace, as this makes sure the inner region of the profile, which contributes the most, gets a sufficient amount of samples.
So we make the substitution $u \equiv \mathrm{ln}x$, $du \equiv 1/x dx$
and rewrite the integral to:
\begin{equation}
4\pi b^{3 - a}\int_{\ln(10^{-5})}^{\ln(b)} 
A \langle N_\mathrm{sat} \rangle 
\exp\left(au
-\frac{\exp(cu)}{b^c}\right)
du.
\end{equation}
For the second integral we rewrite the integrand using the substitution 
$q \equiv \exp[(-x/b)^c]$, $dx = -\frac{b}{c}\left( \frac{x}{b}\right)^{1-c} dq$, $x=-b(\ln q)^{-c}$:
\begin{equation}
-4\pi\int_{\exp(-1)}^{\exp[-(5/b)^c]}
A \langle N_\mathrm{sat} \rangle 
\frac{b}{c}\left( \ln q\right)^{q-2c}
q
\;dq.
\end{equation}
